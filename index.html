Software

The Software Team consisting of Jerald Yik, Lim Xiao Qi & Joyce Thong spent countless hours researching on Speech-To-Text (STT) algorithms (with Jasper), the workings of Raspberry Pi & the ReSpeaker module. Machine Learning models are also implemented to detect and differentiate different types of sounds (car horn etc). With these software designs and models as mentioned above, molded towards desired functionalities, ReVerb is born.

Please turn on sound to view the full content of the videos.

Jasper First Attempt

Successfully booting up main Jasper module for the first time.

Testing out Motor

Utilising the Jasper module as a switch for the motor. Upon picking up certain input words, the Jasper module will trigger certain sub-modules to supply voltage to the motor.

Testing Out Jasper with ReSpeaker's DOA functionality using LED

Testing out ReSpeaker's Directional of Arrival (DOA) functionality. Based on the DOA of certain sounds, the Jasper module will generate the respective output. In this case, when the required sound is coming from the left of the ReSpeaker, the blue LED will light up. Likewise, when the required sound is coming from the right, the yellow LED will light up.

Testing out Voltage Variation

Utilising the in-built algorithms to vary voltage supplied to GPIO pins in the Raspberry Pi, we are able to vary the voltage supplied to the respective LED lights, and hence their brightness, depending on the modules triggered. This will be determined by the respective sounds embedded within algorithms in different sub-modules.

Hardware

The Hardware Team consisting of Colin Loh and Chiang Kai Jun worked around many constraints to formulate the ideal physical prototype to showcase our solution. Countless hours and many iterations later, we arrived at a design we were satisfied with. Here is our journey.

Initial Ideation Sketch

This sketch was done during the ideation stage. From our idea, we came up with a lo-fi, barebones mock-up to gauge public perceptions, as well as to assess the feasibility of the design.

Visual Prototype

Once we decided that our idea was feasible, we came up with a visual prototype, exploring different ways a user might wear and interact with it. It included stereo microphones for Direction of Arrival (DOA) detection, and stereo vibrators to alert the user of the direction of sound.
We decided to have 2 separate working modules, the Main Control Unit (MCU) containing the processors and stereo microphones, and the Peripheral Alert Device (PAD) to alert the user of his/her surroundings.

First Working Prototype

The Raspberry Pi case went through 3 iterations to the final product. This was the first case. It was compact and functional, But Prof Teoh’s input was that the design was uninspiring and bland, urging us to add a few organic curves in a nod to Italian Design. With this, we went back to the drawing board for the second prototype.

Second Working Prototype

Our second case was inspired by the shape of the Lamborghini logo, and Prof Teoh said the design was ‘cool’, which affirmed our design direction. However, we found that the case was far too large to be practical. We thus made one last iteration, to find the right balance in compactness and style.

Third Model Module

The last prototype fits the Raspberry Pi and battery in a compact case, while also maintaining some curves. We decided to proceed with this for our working prototype.

Spectacle Module

The spectacle design for the device allows easier adoption among the masses, in contrast to usual hearing aids which are more conspicuous. The positioning of the vibration module would be well-placed in contact with the Mastoid bone, providing the highest sensitivity to users. Taking inspiration from Italian distinct designs, we decided with a striking red and black combination.

ReSpeaker Housing Module

One design limitation was that the ReSpeaker microphone array had to be positioned on the XY plane for the DOA functionality to work. With this in mind, we implemented the ReSpeaker on a cap that the user can wear for our working prototype.

3D-Printing Process

The designs were done on Solidworks and Shapr3D. They were then printed on a PRUSA i3 Mk3 3D printer using PLA.
